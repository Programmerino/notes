The real North Korean nuclear threat is its noncompliance with safety standards
A mishap at a nuclear facility could have significant consequences for neighboring South Korea, China, Russia, and Japan
Kevin Shepard March 5, 2020
SHARE
SHARE
Kevin Shepard March 5, 2020
Image: NK News

Views expressed in Opinion articles are exclusively the authors’ own and do not represent those of NK News.

North Korean nuclear ambitions threaten the security and stability of the peninsula and the region. The threat stems not only from the possibility of a nuclear strike but from the probability of predictable and preventable facility malfunctions that will destroy the local environment and devastate the nearby populations.

The international community often overlooks this most dangerous aspect of the North Korean nuclear program because of a hyper-vigilant media focus on weapons development.

While North Korean nuclear weapons are without a doubt a threat to the region, the most-likely crisis is an environmental and humanitarian disaster caused by a failure in the North’s nuclear program brought about by their refusal to adhere to international safety standards and exacerbated by the regime’s inability to coordinate assistance from outside the country.

This stems from both fears of external influence and from lack of experience coordinating with nuclear crisis response teams that are inevitably tied to national governments, even if operating in the nuclear energy private sector.

Available international assistance exists, as was seen in response to Japan’s Fukushima Daiichi nuclear disaster in 2011.

Unfortunately, because North Korea is not thought to have the capability to respond to a nuclear disaster and is not expected to transparently seek assistance to any such crisis, the political panic in the region, and the associated negative impact on national economies and international relations, is likely to dwarf the actual humanitarian and environmental impact.

Fukushima Daiichi was the world’s latest nuclear meltdown, but not the first. Lessons have been learned and now standards are in place and specialists are trained to prevent and react to nuclear crises.

These standards and specialists are not adhered to or offered access by the North Korean regime. That constitutes an imminent threat to regional security.

North Korea has pursued a nuclear program for decades garnering intelligence from allies but didn’t implement safeguards other national programs recognized as necessary.

It plowed through the resistance of Cold-War allies in Berlin, Moscow, Prague, and even Budapest, where concerns were voiced over safety standards and facility management. Resistance was originally rooted in Moscow’s adherence to international standards and the Soviet satellite-state network that allowed Russia to influence its allies’ decisions.

North Korea reached out beyond the Cold War Soviet satellite alliance network and traded military secrets with Pakistan from the 1970s. With the implosion of the Soviet Union in the early 1990s and North Korea’s termination of any cooperation with and oversight by the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) in 2009, its nuclear program development has continued without the safety-net provided by experience and standards upheld by allied and international organizations.

Pyongyang-Islamabad nuclear collaboration accelerated, and North Korea advanced enrichment programs based on A. Q. Khan-developed technologies.

Neighboring countries — even like-minded governments — saw the risk and offered help with the establishment and implementation of safe practices, but were shunned by Pyongyang.

North Korea’s nuclear program has now, in the last decade, reached a point where major powers around the world have reevaluated the threat from North Korea. This includes both the direct risk from nuclear capabilities and more the challenging conventional and non-conventional threats borne of North Korea’s confidence in its nuclear deterrent that allows it to provocatively threaten peninsular and regional stability in order to extract concessions.

North Korea’s nuclear deterrent “changes the strategic dynamics with its neighbors,” according to one prominent nuclear scientist. Even China, North Korea’s most powerful post-Cold War ally, has joined the international community as a nation threatened by North Korean nuclear development.

China’s interests are not only put at risk by the destabilizing provocations the North’s nuclear deterrent emboldens Pyongyang to conduct. Chinese seismologists have been monitoring the effects of North Korean underground nuclear tests, at one point leading Beijing to implement an extensive radiation-monitoring network and even order evacuations in the China-North Korea border region of Jilin Province.

A challenge to international cooperation regarding concerns of regional instability instigated by North Korea has been the different perspectives on and prioritization of Pyongyang’s policies.

When planning against revisionist states such as North Korea, we look for both the most dangerous and the most likely scenarios.

While a nuclear strike by the Korean People’s Army would certainly qualify as “most dangerous”, what is much more likely, and deserving of planning and attention, is a crisis on the peninsula spreading to the ROK, China, Russia, and even Japan, that stems from a malfunction or catastrophic disaster at a nuclear facility that North Korea is unable to contain.

Lessons learned from Chernobyl, Rhode Island, and Fukushima inform us of the dangers associated with the North Korean nuclear program — not the threat from its weapons, but rather the threat stemming from the nuclear program’s very existence.

The North’s program is overseen by a rump regime without the resources or wherewithal to safely operate a nuclear program, and without the political confidence to accept assistance from more qualified nuclear states.

Even Chinese nuclear scientists have been turned away frustrated by the North Korean regime’s irresponsible operations. At least one North Korean nuclear test shook buildings in China and caused Beijing authorities to rush atmospheric and geologic testing.

Chinese geologists warn North Korean nuclear testing sends contaminates over the border and its main underground test facility needs to be monitored for leaking radiation following its likely collapse after a 2018 test.

Moscow’s view, while less impactful to regional security, is similar to Beijing’s. Russia has no interest in nuclear proliferation and gains nothing from an unsafe nuclear revisionist state on its border.

North Korea’s nuclear program also poses an additional threat to regional stability.

While other nations have provided unfortunate examples of the vulnerability of nuclear programs, at least their political systems have managed to respond and to recover. This is in large part because those countries did not base their legitimacy on the existence of a nuclear program.

North Korea has done just that, even cementing its nuclear power status in its constitution. A catastrophic mishap at a North Korean nuclear facility — be it civilian power, government research, or military weapons development — would be devastating to the Kim Jong Un regime, and the regime’s response would be threatening to the stability and security of the entire region.

Imagine the scenario: a flailing regime failing to adequately respond to an emergency at a nuclear facility, and regional neighbors challenged with environmental threats, humanitarian concerns, calls to intervene, and a North Korean regime likely feeling the need to respond to perceived weakness.

North Korea’s failure to maintain safety standards could lead to regional military conflict simply through Pyongyang’s refusal to adhere to international standards.

How we view the North Korean nuclear program informs how we plan to respond to a crisis. The most likely crisis we will face is a catastrophic humanitarian disaster caused by a failure from Pyongyang to adhere to standards.

It is up to today’s planners and decision-makers whether the response to this disaster is a coordinated international response by capable teams and governments with the capacity to support, or an ad hoc reaction that risks misunderstandings, conflicts, and increased tensions.
