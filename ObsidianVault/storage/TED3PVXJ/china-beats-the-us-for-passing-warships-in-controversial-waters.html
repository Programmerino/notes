China beats the US for passing warships in controversial waters

-

March 11, 2020
8
0
- Advertisment -
Share
Facebook
Twitter
Pinterest
WhatsApp

    Beijing (AFP) – Beijing accused the United States of a “provocative” act on Wednesday by sending a warship to controversial territorial waters in the South China Sea.

    The USS McCampbell missile destroyer sailed near the Paracel Islands without permission on Tuesday, according to the People’s Liberation Army.

    The Paracel Islands are a chain of controversial islands and reefs in the South China Sea that are claimed by China, Taiwan and Vietnam.

    “Under the guise of” freedom of navigation, “the United States has repeatedly tensed its muscles, has been provocative and has caused problems in the South China Sea,” said Colonel Li Huamin, spokesman for the PLA Southern Theater Command.

    “This is a hegemonic act that violates international law and … threatens the peace and stability of the South China Sea.”

    The PLA Navy forces tracked and identified the ship before warning it to leave.

    The 7th Navy of the US Navy confirmed the freedom of navigation.

    “Illegal and far-reaching maritime claims in the South China Sea pose an unprecedented threat to the freedom of the ocean,” said Commander Reann Mommsen, spokeswoman for the U.S. 7th Fleet, in an emailed statement.

    “With this operation, the United States has shown that these waters are beyond what China can legitimately claim to be its territorial sea, and that China’s claimed baselines around the Paracel Islands are not compatible with international law.”

    In recent years, Beijing has strengthened its territorial claims in the South China Sea by building numerous artificial islands and a strong military presence, making it a focal point for geopolitical tensions.

    The area is home to large natural oil and gas reserves and some of the busiest waterways in the world and is subject to numerous territorial claims from countries such as Indonesia, Malaysia, the Philippines and Brunei.

    Collin Koh, a maritime security researcher at Nanyang Technological University in Singapore, said it was rare that China and not the United States were the first to publicize freedom of navigation.

    “This reflects Beijing’s current policy of emphasizing that the PLA is always ready to respond to external overruns, even if China is dealing with the corona virus,” said Koh.
