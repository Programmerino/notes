US-Saudi Relationship to Remain Centred on Containing Iran

4 March 2020 Onna Evdokimoff, Research Assistant, Indo-Pacific Research Programme
  • Print
  • Email

Background

Despite the occasional hurdle, the past decades have seen the United States-Saudi Arabia relationship grow stronger. Common interests have brought mutual economic and security benefits, primarily in military collaboration and countering their common adversary, Iran. Saudi oil exports to the US and American arms sales to Saudi Arabia have underpinned the relationship. Saudi Arabia’s de-facto leader, Crown Prince, Mohammed bin Salman, has tested US-Saudi relations of late, however, by arresting a number of people in the Kingdom as part of the anti-corruption operations that he introduced in 2017. Contributing events, including the 2018 murder of Washington Post journalist Jamal Khashoggi at the Saudi embassy in Istanbul and Saudi Arabia’s attacks on civilians in its war against Iranian-backed proxies in Yemen have combined with suspected Iranian attacks on Saudi oil and gas installations in 2019 and the recent assassination of Iranian General Qassem Soleimani by the US in Iraq to heighten geopolitical tensions in the Middle East.

Although the friction in the US-Saudi relationship could lead to future uncertainties, the US will reinforce its relationship with Saudi Arabia by working towards containing Iran while maintaining its economic ties with the (slowly) liberalising Kingdom.

 

Comment

Iran-Saudi-US: A Political Triad

Arriving in the Saudi capital, Riyadh, on 19 February, US Secretary of State Michael Pompeo held separate meetings with King Salman and his son, Crown Prince Mohammed, to discuss matters of security, Iran and human rights.

US President Trump’s withdrawal from the Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA, the Iran Nuclear Deal) in 2018 saw harsh and restrictive sanctions re-imposed on Iran. Those sanctions extended the penalties on those countries that continued to trade with Tehran. Iran has since suffered a deep recession and has criticised the US for “economic terrorism”, while President Trump maintains his commitment to applying “maximum pressure” on Iran until it is prepared to re-negotiate the terms of the JCPOA; the details of any such “negotiations” remain vague, however. Iran’s increasingly desperate situation could motivate a stronger Iranian-Russian alliance and Russian military assistance to Iran could further provoke the turbulent geopolitics of the Middle East.

Saudi Arabia’s decision not to retaliate to the suspected Iranian attack on the Saudi Aramco facilities amid its sustained conflict with Iran-backed insurgents in Yemen underscores its economic expenses and the increased risk to the global energy supply chain should hostilities intensify.

Human Rights and Saudi Arabia

President Trump’s resolve to “starve” Iran into submission could itself be perceived as a human rights violation. There is, however, no suggestion of any relaxation of the sanctions in place against it. Mr Pompeo stated upon his departure from Saudi Arabia to Oman that, ‘We certainly talked about our effort to counter Iranian malign influence’, confirming the importance both Washington and Riyadh attach to Iran.

Whether the issue of human rights in the Kingdom was discussed to the degree that Mr Pompeo had originally pledged to do is uncertain. Saudi Arabia maintains “very high” government restrictions concerning freedoms of speech, religion, dress codes and other social customs. The conservative country abides by strict codes and practices derived from the Wahhabist interpretation of Islam.

Currently, Saudi Arabia has jailed a number of US citizens including Badr al-Ibrahim, a physician and writer; women’s rights activist Salah al-Haidar and Walid Fitaihi, a Boston physician, who has been held since 2017. In the matter of murdered Saudi journalist and critic Jamal Khashoggi, Prince Mohammed has been exonerated from any direct involvement, although the US Central Intelligence Agency concluded that the Prince gave the order to eliminate Khashoggi. In his reaction to that finding, President Trump absolved the Crown Prince of complicity in the absence of a confirmed connection to the murder.

The focus of US-Saudi relations will, therefore, continue to be on constraining Iran. In closing his three-day visit to the Kingdom, Mr Pompeo said in relation to Crown Prince Mohammed’s “anti-corruption” programme that, ‘leaders are going to act in their country’s interests.’ Such endorsement by both the President and his top diplomat indicates that the US-Saudi relationship is not likely to be deeply vexed by the issue of human rights reforms in the Kingdom.
